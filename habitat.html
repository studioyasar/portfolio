Habitat for Humanity
UX/UI Case Study/ Responsive Web Design
 
 
 
Project Overview
Habitat for Humanity is a global nonprofit housing organization working in local communities across all 50 states in the U.S. and in approximately 70 countries. Habitat’s vision is of a world where everyone has a decent place to live. This project covers a practical, easy and quick booking process for volunteers as weel as a minor style guide to contemplate the Habitat for Humanity’s mission.
 
`
 
Background
Users currently aren’t connecting with Habitat for Humanity’s website due to an inundation of information that is unclear and unorganized. Users that visit the website in search of volunteer opportunities in particular find that the navigation flow to get to the correct information is confusing.
 
`
 
Solution

Redesign and optimize the website to create an engaging and productive experience for the users, so that they are more inclined to volunteer and reach their goals as well as helping their community.

 
`
 
Hybrid User Testings
We took a hybrid approach to our initial user research interviews by conducting a combination of task testing of the existing website as well as asking users interview questions. This helped us gain a better understanding of each user’s familiarity with the website and organization prior to the user test. This approach also helped us to learn what each user liked and disliked regarding the existing website’s usability, flow and engagement
 
`
 
Affinity Diagram

Key Insights:

  • Users aren’t connecting (emotionally) with the website
  • Navigation menu is confusing due to verbage and an abundance of secondary navigation categories is overwhelming.
  • There’s a lot of content available on the website, it just needs to be refined and better organized
  • It’s confusing to get to volunteer options and/or sign up page which can be frustrating/discouraging for the user.

Card Sorting

During user testing many users found the navigation menu to be confusing due to the wording used as well as an overload of subcategories in the secondary navigation. Our goal was to simplify and make the main navigation more relatable, intuitive and also help form a connection with the organization by using headings such as “ Who We Are” What We Do”, etc.

 
`
 
Site Maps

Card sorting helped us move into our Site Map and decide to move some items that didn’t need to be in the main navigation to our footer. We also incorporated additional information that a user may need or want to find but wasn’t imperative for it to be in the main content of the website.

User Testing

Five users were tested and they found the “Our Goal” and “We Need Your Support” sections on the homepage confusing. This helped us decide to eliminate “Our Goal” and display the mission at the forefront of the layout and reworded “ We Need Your Support” to “Other Ways To Give”. The Accessibility, Help & Contact Us items were too visually similar in the navigation menu. We thought it would then be best to change these to “icons with text” to differentiate them from the menu items. We learned that planning was more important to users vs. demographics in the filters which led to our decision to move planning categories to the top of the filters. Users liked the option of being able to apply or save on the “Project Page”, however, the “Save” button was a bit confusing as they were unclear what they were saving. We decided to incorporate a heart icon on the project page so the user could click the heart to save to their “Saved Projects”

Iterations and Final Thoughts

Filter and organize information in other parts of the website that are essential. In particular, the Donation page as there are so many ways to donate that it may be helpful to simplify, condense and display that information in way that doesn’t feel overwhelming for users. Create a messaging platform within the site. For instance, if a volunteer wants to direct message regarding a particular project or connecting with other volunteers. Incorporate notes and calendar section within a users profile so they may keep their notes, project dates, etc within their profile. As our User Persona is in high school, we focused on mobile, began translating that information into Desktop but were not able to see it through - we hope to continue working to convert the mobile version flow to desktop.
